Most of what community public health nurses do is help people decide to change something — and then support them through it. The four headline topics on this site (smoking, weight, falls, mental health) are different applications of the same small set of frameworks. Learn the engine once and the topics get easier.

1. MECC — Making Every Contact Count OHID

MECC is the NHS / Office for Health Improvement & Disparities approach: every routine contact (a dressing change, a blood pressure check, a school visit) is an opportunity for a brief, opportunistic conversation about health behaviour. It is not a lecture. It is not a long intervention. It is not pushing leaflets. It's a 30–60-second open door, offered every time, by everyone.

The principle behind MECC is simple but counter-intuitive: small, repeated nudges from people the public trusts (nurses, midwives, allied health professionals) accumulate. One conversation is unlikely to change behaviour; conversations across many contacts shift population norms. Because community PH nurses have multiple contacts with the same families and clients, you are particularly well placed to deliver MECC well.

Very Brief Advice — the 30-second template

The classic three-step structure was developed for smoking cessation and is now used across topics:

  1. Ask — establish and record the behaviour. "Do you smoke?" / "How are you sleeping?" / "Do you drink alcohol?"
  2. Advise — the most effective evidence-based route. "The best way to quit is with a combination of behavioural support and stop-smoking medicines — about three times more likely to succeed than going it alone."
  3. Act — offer a referral or signposting today, ideally same-day where opt-out is supported. Even if they decline, the act of offering matters.
Worked example. A practice nurse is doing an annual review with Mrs J, 58, who has hypertension. The review covers BP and bloods. As Mrs J is leaving, the nurse says: "Mrs J, I noticed your record says you smoke. (Ask) Stopping is the single biggest thing you could do to lower your blood-pressure risk. The best way to quit is with our local stop smoking service plus stop-smoking medicines — three times more likely to work than going it alone. (Advise) I can refer you today, would that be okay? (Act)" — 35 seconds.

Common pitfall

Skipping Act. "I'll think about it" without an offer of support is a missed referral. The default in many services is now opt-out referral: you tell the patient you're referring them and they can opt out if they want. This raises uptake substantially.

2. Motivational Interviewing — OARS Miller & Rollnick

Motivational interviewing (MI) is a way of having conversations that helps the person talk themselves into change. It is not persuasion, advice-giving in disguise, or "being nice". It is a specific skill set with a substantial evidence base across smoking, alcohol, weight, medication adherence and mental health.

Students are not expected to be MI-trained therapists. You are expected to recognise the four core micro-skills and use them where you can — they make every conversation better, even brief ones.

SkillWhat it sounds likeWhy it matters
Open questions"What's a typical day like for your eating?" rather than "Do you eat breakfast?"Yes/no questions close conversations. Open questions invite the person to think and tell you something useful.
Affirmations"It took something to come to this appointment today." Genuine, specific, not flattery.Reinforces self-efficacy. Different from praise — it names a strength.
Reflections"So you want to cut down, but evenings are when you really struggle."Mirrors their meaning. Shows you've listened. Slows the conversation. Invites them to clarify.
Summaries"Let me check I've got this right — you said…"Pulls the conversation together. Shows accountability. Gives them a chance to correct you.

The "righting reflex"

The single biggest mistake nurses make in behaviour-change conversations is rushing to fix. Someone says "I want to lose weight" and the nurse instantly says "have you tried Slimming World?" That's the righting reflex — the urge to set someone right. It feels helpful. It usually isn't, because you're solving a problem they haven't yet fully described.

Reflections are the antidote. Instead of jumping to advice, reflect back what you heard: "It sounds like weight has been on your mind for a while." Now they have to engage with their own statement, which is exactly the work of change.

Change talk vs sustain talk

People in any process of change voice both — sometimes in the same sentence. MI calls this ambivalence and treats it as normal, not as resistance.

  • Change talk: "I should stop." / "It's affecting my kids." / "I tried last year and got 3 weeks in."
  • Sustain talk: "It's the only thing I do for me." / "Nothing's worked before." / "I'll cut down when work calms down."

Reflect change talk back to amplify it. Don't argue with sustain talk — arguing entrenches it. Sustain talk is information about the obstacles, not a reason to give up on the conversation.

Source: Miller WR & Rollnick S. Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change, 3rd ed. (Guilford Press, 2013).

3. The COM-B model Michie et al.

For any behaviour to happen, three things must be in place. If a behaviour isn't happening, work out which is missing. This stops you from misdiagnosing the problem (and offering the wrong intervention).

QuestionIf missing, you might…
CapabilityDo they know how / are they physically able? Includes physical capability and psychological capability (knowledge, skills, self-efficacy).Teach. Demonstrate. Build confidence with small wins.
OpportunityDoes their environment allow it? Includes physical opportunity (access, time, money, kit) and social opportunity (norms, family, culture).Address the environment. Refer to other services. Involve family.
MotivationDo they want to? Includes reflective motivation (beliefs, plans) and automatic motivation (habit, emotion, identity).Use MI. Explore values. Decisional balance.
Worked example. Mr A wants to lose weight. He has Type 2 diabetes. He understands what he should eat (capability ✓). He wants to (motivation ✓). But he's a long-distance lorry driver who eats at service stations, sleeps in his cab, and has no fridge (opportunity ✗). Telling him to "eat more vegetables" misses the point — the intervention is a conversation about what he can do given his work, not what he should do in an idealised kitchen.

Source: Michie S, van Stralen MM, West R. The behaviour change wheel. Implementation Science 2011;6:42.

4. Stages of Change (Transtheoretical Model) Prochaska & DiClemente

People don't move from "doesn't want to change" to "changed" in one step. The stages are not strictly linear — people loop back, especially through relapse. Match your intervention to the stage:

StageWhat they're sayingWhat helps
Pre-contemplation"I'm fine, I don't need to."Plant seeds. Don't push. Validate the contact. Build trust for next time.
Contemplation"I should, but…"Explore ambivalence. Decisional balance — costs and benefits of changing vs not changing.
Preparation"Yes, soon."Concrete next step. SMART action. Refer to support service. Anticipate obstacles.
Action"I've started."Reinforce. Troubleshoot. Plan for high-risk situations. Celebrate small wins.
Maintenance"It's been a while."Relapse prevention. Identify triggers and protective routines.
Lapse / relapse"I slipped."Normalise. Re-engage without shame. A lapse is information, not failure.

Common pitfall

Treating a pre-contemplator like a preparer. If you offer a quit-date diary to someone who hasn't even acknowledged a problem, you'll lose them. Match your intervention to where they actually are — not where you wish they were.

Source: Prochaska JO, DiClemente CC. Stages and processes of self-change of smoking. J Consult Clin Psychol 1983;51(3):390–5.

5. The inequalities lens

Behaviour-change conversations that ignore context come across as judgemental and miss the point. Two frameworks every UK community PH nurse should know:

  • Marmot's social determinants — the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work and age shape health more than individual behaviour. Income, housing, work, early-years experience, education, neighbourhood — these are the playing field.
  • NHS Core20PLUS5 — focuses on the most deprived 20% of the population plus locally identified groups (Core20PLUS), against five clinical priorities — including smoking cessation, severe mental illness, hypertension, chronic respiratory disease and early cancer diagnosis (adults).

On placement, this lens means: before you offer advice, ask what's going on for the person. Cost-of-living, housing quality, work patterns, caring responsibilities, language, immigration status, racism, disability — these are not excuses, they are the playing field. The same VBA, delivered without that awareness, can read as nagging.

Sources: Marmot M et al. Health Equity in England: The Marmot Review 10 Years On (Institute of Health Equity, 2020); NHS England Core20PLUS5 — adults (2021) and CYP (2022).

Putting it together — the 60-second decision tree

  1. Where is this person on Stages of Change? — that tells you the broad approach.
  2. If they're not changing, which COM-B element is missing? — that tells you what to address.
  3. Use OARS to listen. — opens the door, builds rapport, surfaces change talk.
  4. Close with VBA. — Ask, Advise, Act, every time.
  5. Through it all, hold the inequalities lens. — what's the playing field?

Smoking cessation

Why smoking cessation matters

Smoking is the leading cause of preventable death and disability in the UK. It causes around 80,000 deaths a year in England alone, and roughly half of all long-term smokers will die from a smoking-related disease. The conditions are familiar — lung cancer, COPD, ischaemic heart disease, stroke, multiple other cancers, peripheral vascular disease — but smoking also drives less obvious harms: type 2 diabetes risk, dementia, fertility, fracture healing, complications after surgery.

Stopping at any age improves life expectancy. Quitting before 40 avoids most of the excess mortality; quitting at 60 still adds years and quality. The gains start fast — within 24 hours, CO levels normalise; within weeks, lung function improves measurably; within a year, coronary risk halves; over 10–15 years it approaches that of a never-smoker.Doll et al.

What good looks like

Every contact records smoking status. Every smoker is offered Very Brief Advice. Every smoker who is interested is referred to a behavioural support service and offered evidence-based pharmacotherapy. The combination is roughly three times more effective than willpower alone. None of this is gatekept; you don't need a "ready" smoker — anyone who smokes is offered the conversation, every time.

Very Brief Advice for smoking — the standard

  1. Ask: "Do you smoke?" — record it. (Cigarettes, roll-ups, vapes, shisha, cannabis with tobacco — all relevant.)
  2. Advise: "The best way to stop is with a combination of behavioural support and stop-smoking medicines — about three times more likely to succeed than going it alone."
  3. Act: "I can refer you to our local stop smoking service today, would that be okay?" Default to opt-out referral if your service supports it — uptake is substantially higher.

Adapted from NCSCT Very Brief Advice; aligned with NICE NG209.

Common pitfall — the willpower script

Saying "you just need to want it enough" is wrong, unhelpful, and unkind. Tobacco dependence is a genuine addiction — nicotine is one of the most addictive substances we know. Telling smokers it's a willpower problem suggests their previous attempts failed because they didn't want it enough. Most smokers have tried multiple times. The evidence is clear: the right support, including pharmacotherapy, makes the difference.

Pharmacotherapy at a glance

Students do not prescribe. Recognise the options so you can answer "what's available?" — and so you know who needs flagging.

Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT)

Replaces the nicotine the smoker is dependent on, without the smoke (which is the main source of harm). Comes in two functional types:

  • Long-acting: patches (16- or 24-hour). Background nicotine through the day.
  • Short-acting: gum, lozenge, mouth spray, nasal spray, inhalator, mouth strip. Address breakthrough cravings within a few minutes.

Combination NRT — patch plus a fast-acting form — is more effective than single NRT. It is first-line in NICE NG209. Many students assume "double dose = bad". The opposite is true: single NRT often under-treats, leading to relapse.

Side effects are usually local (skin reactions to patches, mouth irritation with gum/lozenge). NRT is safer than smoking in pregnancy when behavioural support alone has not worked.

Varenicline

A prescription oral medication that partially activates nicotinic receptors and blocks reinforcement from smoking. Highly effective. Pfizer's branded version Champix was withdrawn from the UK in 2021 due to a nitrosamine impurity issue; varenicline has since returned to the UK market under different manufacturers and is no longer sold as Champix. As always, check current SPC and your formulary. Not used in pregnancy or under 18.

Bupropion (Zyban)

An oral antidepressant licensed for smoking cessation. Less commonly used than NRT or varenicline. Several contraindications including history of seizures and eating disorders; check the BNF. Not in pregnancy or under 18.

Cytisine

A plant-derived nicotinic partial agonist used in many countries for decades. Recommended by NICE NG209 as a quit aid. UK availability has been variable; check what your service stocks.

E-cigarettes / vapes

Not a licensed medicine, but recommended by NICE and OHID as a quit aid for adult smokers. The expert consensus is that vaping is substantially less harmful than smoking — though "less harmful" is not "harmless", and the long-term picture is still maturing. Vapes should not be promoted to non-smokers, and not to under-18s. In pregnancy, many UK community services now offer NRT and vaping as equal quit-aid options alongside specialist behavioural support — see Pregnancy below.

Always check the current BNF for indications, doses and contraindications. Pharmacotherapy availability changes — confirm with your service before saying anything definite.

Specific populations — pathways to know

Pregnancy SMP / NICE / OHID

  • Smoking in pregnancy is a leading cause of stillbirth, preterm birth, low birthweight, sudden infant death and other harms — stopping is the single biggest thing that improves outcomes.
  • National policy: CO monitoring at every antenatal appointment. CO ≥4 ppm typically triggers offer of specialist support; thresholds vary.
  • Refer to a specialist maternal stop smoking service — generic services are not the right fit.
  • NRT and vaping are both offered as quit-aid options in pregnancy alongside specialist behavioural support. Many UK community services now treat them as equal options and follow the woman's preference, on the principle that the priority is stopping smoking and the right tool is the one she'll stick with. NRT is licensed; vaping is unlicensed but supported. For NRT, use the lowest effective dose; intermittent forms (gum, spray) are often preferred over 24-hr patches. Confirm specifics with the prescriber and follow your local pathway.
  • Varenicline and bupropion are not used in pregnancy.

Severe mental illness (SMI) NICE NG209 / RCPsych

Clozapine — fatal-toxicity risk if smoking stops without dose review

Tobacco smoke (not nicotine) induces the liver enzyme CYP1A2. When a smoker on clozapine stops smoking — including switching to vaping or NRT, or being admitted to a smoke-free ward — clearance falls and plasma clozapine can rise rapidly to toxic levels. Clozapine toxicity can cause seizures, arrhythmias and death. The prescriber and mental health team must be informed before or at the point of stopping, so the dose can be reviewed and levels monitored. The same mechanism affects olanzapine (less acutely dangerous but dose may need adjustment). Other CYP1A2-metabolised drugs (e.g. caffeine, theophylline, some others) are also affected.

  • Smoking rates in people with SMI are roughly 2–3× the general population.
  • People with SMI die on average 15–20 years earlier than the general population, and smoking is the single largest contributor.
  • Stopping smoking is feasible in this population with good support — old assumptions that it would worsen mental health have been refuted.
  • Refer to specialist support; "smokefree NHS" trust policies should be enabling, not punitive.

Children and young people

  • Dedicated CYP pathways — adult services aren't designed for the developmental and confidentiality needs of under-18s.
  • Vaping in young people is a public health concern in its own right (advertising, flavours, nicotine dependence in non-smokers).
  • Varenicline and bupropion are not used in under-18s.
  • Always think about the household — exposure to second-hand smoke in children is itself a health harm.

The CO monitor — how to use it well

CO breath testing is a simple, immediate-feedback tool. The smoker exhales into the device after a 15-second breath hold; the reading appears in ppm and as % carboxyhaemoglobin. A few notes:

  • A typical non-smoker is ≤4 ppm. Recently smoked >10 ppm. Heavy smokers can be 20–40+.
  • It is a tool for the smoker, not for you to pass judgement. Used well it shows them their own risk in numbers and lets them watch it fall as they cut down or quit.
  • A high reading in someone who says they don't smoke can also mean recent passive exposure — or, rarely, faulty domestic appliances. Consider a gas-safety check if the picture doesn't fit.

Inequalities lens

Smoking is one of the largest drivers of health inequality in England. Adults in routine and manual occupations smoke at roughly twice the rate of managerial/professional roles. People with SMI, in homelessness, in prison, and in some long-term-condition groups smoke at much higher rates than the general population. Smoking-related illness sits disproportionately on the most deprived communities and is the single biggest contributor to the life-expectancy gap.

Practically: the smoker in front of you may have far less margin than you to "just stop", and may have stopped before only to relapse under stress. Use that as data, not as evidence they don't want to. Treat the conversation as a health-equity intervention, not a lifestyle nag.

Sources: ONS Adult smoking habits in the UK (annual); OHID; NHS England Core20PLUS5.

  • Referral made — to where, when, with consent.

Falls prevention

Summary only

Below is a condensed reference. The full learning module (Objectives, Read, Apply, Test, Reflect) is in development.

What good looks like

Falls are not "just part of getting older". A community-based falls assessment uses a multifactorial approach to identify modifiable risk factors and offer evidence-based interventions — most centrally, structured strength and balance training.

Evidence in 60 seconds

  • About 1 in 3 adults aged 65+ and 1 in 2 aged 80+ fall at least once a year. NICE CG161
  • Multifactorial assessment + targeted interventions reduces fall rate.
  • Strength and balance training (e.g. Otago, FaME) is the highest-evidence single intervention.

The multifactorial assessment — what to look for

DomainWhat to ask / observe
Falls historyNumber, mechanism, where, what they were doing, injuries, fear of falling
Gait & balanceTimed Up and Go; able to stand from chair without arms?
StrengthSit-to-stand x5; functional reach
VisionLast sight test; bifocals on stairs (a known risk)
Cognition4AT or similar if delirium suspected
ContinenceUrgency & nocturia drive night-time falls
CardiovascularLying/standing BP — postural hypotension
MedicationsPolypharmacy >4; sedatives, opioids, antihypertensives, anticholinergics
Feet & footwearPain, deformity, slippers without backs
Bone healthPrevious fragility fracture; FRAX risk
Home environmentLighting, rugs, stairs, bathroom
AlcoholAUDIT-C screening may still be useful if alcohol could be contributing

Red flags — escalate

  • Head injury on anticoagulants: low threshold for A&E.
  • Loss of consciousness / collapse: not a "fall" — needs syncope work-up.
  • Postural drop ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic, especially with symptoms.
  • Suspected fragility fracture: hip pain after a fall, inability to weight-bear, shortened/externally rotated leg.
  • Acute confusion after a fall — consider delirium, intracranial bleed, sepsis.
  • Frequent falls with new fatigue / breathlessness — consider cardiac cause, anaemia.

How you talk about weight matters

Weight stigma is a documented health harm and can drive avoidance of healthcare. Use neutral, person-first language. Ask before discussing weight. Focus on health behaviours rather than the number on the scale.

BMI — with caveats

25.0–29.9Overweight
30.0–34.9Obesity class I

  • Lower thresholds apply for adults of South Asian, Chinese, other Asian, Middle Eastern, Black African or African-Caribbean family background — overweight from 23 and obesity from 27.5. NICE CG189 (2023 update)
  • BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. Use waist-to-height ratio (aim <0.5) as a complementary marker.

Red flags

  • Eating disorder features — restriction, binge-purge cycles, body-image distress, rapid weight loss. NICE NG69. Refer to mental health / specialist eating disorder service.
  • Children / young people: NCMP letter pathway and trained school nurse / community paediatric services.
  • Unintentional weight loss in older adults: think frailty, malignancy, depression, dementia.
  • Pregnancy: separate guidance; do not aim for weight loss.

Mental health & wellbeing

Read the Risk & escalation page first. If a client discloses suicidal thoughts now, stay with them and follow your service escalation pathway.

What "low intensity" means in community

Most community public health work in mental health is at the identification, brief intervention and signposting level. You are not delivering therapy. You are: noticing distress, having a kind and skilled conversation, screening using validated tools where appropriate, and connecting people with the right service.

Frameworks worth knowing

  • 5 Ways to Wellbeing — Connect, Be Active, Take Notice, Keep Learning, Give. Useful for low-intensity prompts. NEF / OHID
  • NHS Talking Therapies (formerly IAPT) — self-referral primary-care service for common mental health problems.
  • Stepped care — Step 1 recognition / psychoeducation; Step 2 low-intensity psychological interventions; Step 3 high-intensity therapy and/or medication; Step 4 crisis / specialist services.

Red flags — non-negotiable

  • Suicidal ideation, plan, intent or means — see Risk & escalation. Ask directly. Asking does not increase risk.
  • Self-harm — assess medical need; signpost. NICE NG225 advises against using risk-stratification tools to gate care.
  • Psychosis features — escalate to GP / crisis team.
  • Perinatal mental health — pregnant or first 12 months postpartum — specific pathway; mental illness is a leading cause of maternal death.
  • Children & young people — separate pathways (CAMHS, school nurse, MHST).

Risk & escalation

If a person is in immediate danger

Call 999. Stay with them. Don't leave to fetch help unless leaving is necessary to get help.

This page is short on purpose. Read it now so you don't have to read it later.

1. Suicide & self-harm

Asking the question

Use clear, direct language. Euphemisms create ambiguity.

  • "Have you been having any thoughts of ending your life?"
  • "How often do those thoughts come?"
  • "Have you thought about how you might do it?" — this is asking about plan.
  • "Do you have access to [the means they mention]?" — asking about means.
  • "What's stopped you so far?" — protective factors.

What raises concern

  • Frequent or intrusive thoughts of suicide
  • A specific plan or rehearsed plan
  • Access to means (medication, ligature, weapons)
  • Recent loss, relationship breakdown, redundancy, criminal proceedings
  • Recent discharge from inpatient mental health care (highest-risk window)
  • Hopelessness, "everyone would be better off without me"
  • Sudden calm after agitation — may indicate decision made
  • Severe sleeplessness, command hallucinations
  • Substance use intoxication
  • Previous attempt — strongest single risk factor

What to do

  1. Stay calm and stay with them. Don't promise confidentiality you can't keep.
  2. If imminent risk — 999 / go to A&E. Do not leave them alone if you can avoid it.
  3. If significant but not imminent — escalate to your practice supervisor / assessor immediately. Crisis team / 111 option 2 (NHS mental health crisis line). Same-day GP. Local authority adult social care if vulnerability concerns.
  4. Self-harm of any severity — assess medical need first; offer pathway in. NICE NG225: do not use risk-stratification scores to gate access.
  5. Safety planning (with someone trained, not solo as a student): a written plan listing warning signs, coping strategies, people/places that distract, people they can ask for help, professionals/agencies, means restriction.
  6. Document everything. What was said, in their words; what you assessed; what you did; what was agreed; who you escalated to; follow-up plan.

Source: NICE NG225 (Self-harm, 2022); Zero Suicide Alliance training; Samaritans guidance.

2. Safeguarding adults Care Act 2014

Section 42 of the Care Act 2014 places a duty on local authorities to make enquiries where an adult:

  1. has needs for care and support, AND
  2. is experiencing, or at risk of, abuse or neglect, AND
  3. as a result of those needs is unable to protect themselves.

Categories: physical, domestic (incl. coercive control), sexual, psychological, financial/material, modern slavery, discriminatory, organisational/institutional, neglect/acts of omission, self-neglect.

What to do: Document specifics (verbatim where possible). Discuss with your supervisor today. Make a safeguarding referral to local authority adult social care per your service procedure. Mental Capacity Act applies — see below. Do not investigate yourself.

3. Safeguarding children Children Act / Working Together

If you are concerned that a child is at risk of significant harm:

  1. Discuss with your service's named safeguarding lead the same day.
  2. Refer to local authority children's social care.
  3. If a child is in immediate danger — 999.
  4. Document fully. Concerns about a colleague go to the Local Authority Designated Officer (LADO).

Categories: physical, emotional, sexual, neglect (HM Government, Working Together to Safeguard Children, 2023). FGM disclosure for under-18s carries a mandatory reporting duty to police (Serious Crime Act 2015).

4. Mental Capacity Act 2005 — the five principles

  1. Presume capacity.
  2. Help the person make their own decision before concluding they can't.
  3. People are entitled to make unwise decisions.
  4. Any act or decision made for someone lacking capacity must be in their best interests.
  5. Choose the least restrictive option.

Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific. The two-stage test: (1) is there an impairment of mind/brain? (2) does it mean they cannot understand, retain, weigh, or communicate the decision? You may need to flag a capacity concern to your supervisor; formal assessment is by an appropriate professional. Don't conflate "made a choice I disagree with" with "lacks capacity".

5. Domestic abuse

Coercive and controlling behaviour is a criminal offence (Serious Crime Act 2015). Routine enquiry only when alone and safely. Never ask in front of a partner or via a family-member translator. National Domestic Abuse Helpline (24h, free): 0808 2000 247. MARAC referral via your service for high-risk cases (DASH risk indicator).

6. Escalation — SBAR

Any escalation phone call uses SBAR. Have your notes ready.

SituationWho you are, where, who the patient is, why you're calling.
BackgroundRelevant history, current presentation.
AssessmentWhat you think is going on; what you've already done.
RecommendationWhat you need from them — and by when.
